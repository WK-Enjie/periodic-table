📖 Key Definitions

🔬 Physical Property ⭐
Observed/measured without forming a new substance. E.g., MP, BP, density, colour, state, conductivity.
⚗️ Chemical Property ⭐
Describes how a substance reacts to form new substances. E.g., reactivity with water/acids, displacement, flammability.
🛡️ Shielding Effect ⭐
Reduction in electrostatic forces of attraction between nucleus and outer electrons caused by inner shells.
🔗 Metallic Bond ⭐
Electrostatic forces of attraction between positive metal ions (cations) and a sea of delocalised electrons.
💨 Intermolecular Forces of Attraction ⭐
Weak forces between molecules. For non-polar: London dispersion forces. More electrons = stronger forces = higher BP.
⚖️ Displacement Reaction ⭐
A more reactive element takes the place of a less reactive one in a compound.
🔄 Redox ⭐
OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss, Reduction Is Gain of electrons.
⚗️ Catalyst ⭐
Increases rate without being chemically changed. Provides alternative pathway with lower activation energy.

📝 Why do Group 1 MPs decrease ↓?
  1. M⁺ ionic radius increases.
  2. Delocalised e⁻ further from M⁺.
  3. Electrostatic forces of attraction become weaker.
  4. Less energy needed → lower MP.

Redox Analysis (Half-Equations)

Taking Cl₂ + 2KBr as an example:

Reduction (Cl₂ gains e⁻)
Cl₂ + 2e⁻ → 2Cl⁻
Chlorine is reduced — it is the oxidising agent
Oxidation (Br⁻ loses e⁻)
2Br⁻ → Br₂ + 2e⁻
Bromide is oxidised — it is the reducing agent
OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss. Reduction Is Gain. Cl₂ gains electrons more readily than Br₂ (more reactive).

Redox in Metal Displacement

Fe + Cu²⁺ → Fe²⁺ + Cu:

Oxidation
Fe → Fe²⁺ + 2e⁻
Iron loses electrons → oxidised (reducing agent)
Reduction
Cu²⁺ + 2e⁻ → Cu
Copper(II) gains electrons → reduced (oxidising agent)
