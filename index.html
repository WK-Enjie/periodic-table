📋 Key Definitions

Essential terms for O-Level 6092. ⭐ = especially important for exams.

🔬 Physical Property ⭐
Observed or measured without changing the substance into a new substance. No chemical reaction. E.g. melting point, boiling point, density, colour, state, hardness, electrical conductivity.
🧪 Chemical Property ⭐
Describes how a substance reacts with other substances to form new substances. A chemical change occurs. E.g. reactivity with water, acids, oxygen; flammability; displacement behaviour.
🔮 Atomic Radius ⭐
The distance from the centre of the nucleus to the outermost electron shell. Measured in picometres (pm). Decreases across a period; increases down a group.
⚡ First Ionisation Energy ⭐
The energy required to remove one electron from each atom in one mole of gaseous atoms to form one mole of gaseous 1+ ions. Increases across; decreases down.
🛡 Shielding Effect ⭐
The reduction in the electrostatic forces of attraction between the nucleus and the outermost electrons caused by inner electron shells. More inner shells = more shielding.
⛏ Metallic Character
The tendency of an element to lose electrons and form positive ions (cations). Increases down a group; decreases across a period.
🔥 Reactivity ⭐
How readily an element undergoes chemical reactions. For metals: how readily they lose electrons. For non-metals: how readily they gain electrons.
➕ Nuclear Charge ⭐
The total positive charge of the nucleus, determined by the number of protons.
🔗 Metallic Bond ⭐
The electrostatic forces of attraction between positive metal ions (cations) and a "sea" of delocalised electrons.
💭 Intermolecular Forces of Attraction ⭐
Weak forces of attraction between molecules. For non-polar molecules: intermolecular forces of attraction (London dispersion forces) caused by temporary dipoles. More electrons = stronger forces = higher MP/BP. For polar molecules: intermolecular forces of attraction (permanent dipole–dipole forces).
〰️ Intermolecular Forces of Attraction (London Dispersion Forces)
A type of intermolecular forces of attraction caused by temporary dipoles in non-polar molecules. Larger atoms/molecules = more electrons = stronger intermolecular forces of attraction (London dispersion forces).
〰️ Intermolecular Forces of Attraction (Permanent Dipole–Dipole Forces)
A type of intermolecular forces of attraction between polar molecules. The δ+ end of one molecule attracts the δ− end of another.
⚔ Displacement Reaction ⭐
A reaction where a more reactive element takes the place of a less reactive element in a compound.
🔄 Redox Reaction ⭐
OIL RIG: Oxidation Is Loss of electrons. Reduction Is Gain of electrons. Both occur simultaneously.
🧬 Catalyst ⭐
A substance that increases the rate of reaction without being chemically changed at the end. Provides an alternative reaction pathway with lower activation energy.

📝 Why do Group 1 MPs decrease down the group?
  1. M⁺ ionic radius increases.
  2. Delocalised e⁻ further from M⁺.
  3. Electrostatic forces of attraction become weaker.
  4. Less energy needed → lower MP.

🔴 Alkali Metals — Group 1

Physical vs Chemical Properties

PHYSICAL

  • Soft — cut with knife
  • Shiny when freshly cut
  • Low density — Li, Na, K float on water
  • Low MPs — decreases down the group
  • Good conductors

CHEMICAL

  • Reactivity increases down the group
  • React with water → hydroxide + H₂
  • React with oxygen → metal oxide
  • React with halogens → metal halides
  • Form 1+ ions
  • Stored under mineral oil

🔵 Halogens — Group 17

Physical vs Chemical Properties

PHYSICAL

  • Diatomic molecules (F₂, Cl₂, Br₂, I₂)
  • Colour gets darker down the group
  • State: gas → liquid → solid down the group
  • MP & BP increase down the group
  • Poor conductors

CHEMICAL

  • Reactivity decreases down the group
  • React with metals → ionic metal halides
  • Displacement: more reactive displaces less reactive
  • Form 1− ions (halide ions)
  • Are oxidising agents
HalogenColourStateBP
F₂Pale yellowGas−188°C

Br₂Red-brownLiquid59°C
I₂Dark grey / purple vapourSolid184°C
📝 Why do halogen BPs increase down the group?
  1. Molecules get larger with more electrons down the group.
  2. Stronger intermolecular forces of attraction (London dispersion forces).
  3. More energy needed to overcome these stronger intermolecular forces of attraction.
  4. ∴ BP increases down the group.
Note: Covalent bonds within molecules do NOT break during boiling. Only intermolecular forces of attraction are overcome.
📝 Why does reactivity decrease down Group 17?
  1. More electron shells → outer shell further from nucleus.
  2. Greater shielding by inner electrons.
  3. Weaker electrostatic forces of attraction between nucleus and incoming electron.
  4. An additional electron is gained less readily.
  5. ∴ Reactivity decreases down the group.

OIL RIG: The halogen being added is the oxidising agent (it gets reduced — gains e⁻). The halide ion being displaced is the reducing agent (it gets oxidised — loses e⁻).

🟣 Noble Gases — Group 18

He, Ne, Ar, Kr, Xe, Rn — extremely stable. Full outer electron shells.

Physical vs Chemical Properties

PHYSICAL

  • Colourless, odourless gases
  • Exist as single atoms (monatomic)
  • Very low MP & BP — increases down the group
  • Non-flammable
  • Poor conductors

CHEMICAL

  • Extremely unreactive (inert)
  • Full outer electron shell
  • Do not form ions or bonds normally
  • No tendency to gain, lose, or share electrons

📝 Why are noble gases unreactive?
  1. Noble gases have a complete (full) outer electron shell.
  2. They have no tendency to gain, lose, or share electrons.
  3. Therefore they do not form bonds or take part in chemical reactions.
  4. They are described as inert.

🟢 Transition Metals — Groups 3–12

Fe, Cu, Au, Pt — strong, versatile, essential for industry.

Physical vs Chemical Properties

PHYSICAL

  • High melting points (except Hg)
  • High density
  • Hard and strong
  • Good conductors
  • Malleable & ductile

CHEMICAL

  • Less reactive than Group 1
  • Form coloured compounds
  • Variable oxidation states
  • Act as catalysts
  • Can undergo displacement reactions
